## Practical Prop-Walk Modeling for a Low-Speed MMG-Style 3DOF Maneuvering Engine

### A. Executive recommendation

Implement **an “astern-biased transverse thrust” prop-walk module** that adds a **propeller-induced lateral force** \(Y_P\) (and the corresponding yaw moment) to your existing MMG-style force balance, with these design goals:

- **Primary activation in astern telegraph** (including the “ahead speed + astern command” stopping quadrant), so your validated ahead behavior is not disturbed by default.
- **Magnitude proportional to the already-computed axial propeller surge force** \(X_P\) (or propeller thrust \(T\)), multiplied by:
  - a **single tunable coefficient** for how “walky” the ship is in reverse, and
  - a **smooth decay function** in \(|J|\) (or, if \(J\) is unavailable, in \(|u|\)) so the effect is strongest near bollard pull / very low speed and weakens as the ship gains sternway.
- **Yaw moment derived from the force lever arm** about your reference point (usually CG):  
  \[
  N_P \approx (x_P-x_{ref})\,Y_P
  \]
  with an **optional small additional empirical correction moment** (default 0) to cover the fact that real “reversing effects” can involve distributed hull pressures (stern and bow contributions), not a single point force. citeturn4view0turn6view0

Why this is the best balance for your project:

- **Realism where it matters**: shiphandling guidance and experiments emphasize that transverse thrust is **most pronounced in astern** and can dominate low-speed turning/“stern kick” during docking and stopping. citeturn44view0turn4view0
- **Minimal data burden**: the model can be driven from values you already compute (\(X_P\), \(u\), \(x_P\)), with only 1–3 new tunables.
- **MMG-clean integration**: standard MMG decomposes forces by hull/rudder/propeller and, in its baseline form, often uses the propeller only for surge \(X_P\). Your gap is precisely adding a pragmatic \(Y_P\) (and thus \(N_P\)). citeturn10view2turn41view0
- **Ahead behavior protection**: setting \(Y_P=0\) in ahead by default matches common maneuvering-code practice (propeller side force often neglected because it is comparatively small in many ahead cases), while still allowing an optional small ahead bias if later desired. citeturn41view0turn44view0

### B. Source-backed theory

#### Practical physical cause of prop-walk

“Prop-walk” (often called **transverse thrust**) is the **side force / yaw tendency associated with a rotating propeller**, especially noticeable during **astern operation at low speed**. Practical shiphandling references describe it as arising from **interaction between hull, propeller, and rudder**, with the effect **more pronounced astern** and capable of pushing the stern laterally (creating a strong yaw). citeturn44view0

In reverse, high-asymmetry flow and pressure fields near the stern can form—one CFD-based stopping study attributes the bow yaw during reversing to **asymmetric pressure distribution on the aft hull** generated by the reversing propeller’s induced flow (“reversing effect”), producing a lateral force that pushes the stern sideways and turns the bow oppositely. citeturn6view0

Experimental work on a reversing ship model shows that, when normalized by propeller thrust, **stern and bow lateral forces** (and their effective longitudinal application points) can be substantial and vary with an “astern advance ratio,” implying that what shiphandlers experience as “prop-walk” is often the **net effect of a flow-induced force system acting on the hull**, not only a “propeller blade” force at a single point. citeturn4view0turn4view1turn4view2

#### How maneuvering literature represents propeller-induced lateral force

Baseline MMG-style modeling (originating from the entity["organization","Maneuvering Modeling Group (MMG)","ship manoeuvring model"] concept and standardized by a committee of the entity["organization","Japan Society of Naval Architects and Ocean Engineers","naval architects society japan"]) decomposes forces into hull, rudder, and propeller components. In the standard formulation documented by entity["people","Hironori Yasukawa","ship manoeuvring researcher"] and entity["people","Yasuo Yoshimura","ship manoeuvring researcher"], the propeller is commonly modeled as **surge force only** \(X_P\) (via thrust deduction and open-water \(K_T(J)\)), while \(Y\) and \(N\) are handled by hull and rudder terms. citeturn10view2turn38view1

This “propeller-only-in-surge” choice is also mirrored in practical MMG-based software documentation: one MMG-based code guide explicitly notes that propeller side force \(Y_P\) and yaw moment \(N_P\) are **neglected** because they are typically smaller than hull and rudder components (in the model’s intended regimes). citeturn41view0

However, propeller forces during maneuvering are also modeled in more general hydrodynamic form as:
\[
T=\rho D^4 n^2 K_T(J), \quad Y=\rho D^4 n^2 K_Y(J),
\]
with \(K_Y\) capturing side force under oblique/rotational conditions, and further semi-empirical alternatives relating \(Y\) to transverse inflow in the propeller plane and torque coefficient. citeturn40view0

For low-speed docking/berthing where propeller/rudder frequently run off-design and across quadrants, review literature on automatic berthing modeling summarizes **four-quadrant propeller representations** that explicitly compute not only \(X_P\) but also \(Y_P\) and \(N_P\) as functions of a propeller “inflow/pitch angle” and resultant inflow speed, using lateral and yaw coefficients \(C_{PY}(\theta_P), C_{PN}(\theta_P)\). citeturn43view0

#### Coordinate and sign conventions in canonical MMG references

The standardized MMG documentation defines a ship-fixed coordinate system with:
- \(x\): forward to bow  
- \(y\): to starboard  
- \(z\): downward  
and drift angle \(\beta = \tan^{-1}(-v_m/u)\). citeturn38view1turn39view0

This is important because prop-walk sign logic is otherwise ambiguous.

### C. Candidate formulas

Below are practical candidate models you can add cleanly to an MMG-like 3DOF force balance. I focus on models that (i) improve low-speed astern realism and (ii) are implementable with few coefficients.

#### Candidate model: constant-fraction transverse thrust (thrust-proportional)

**Formula (force-only, moment from lever):**
\[
Y_P = k_{PW}\,|X_P|\,s,\qquad N_P=(x_P-x_{ref})\,Y_P
\]

**Definitions**
- \(X_P\) [N]: your existing propeller surge contribution (positive ahead, negative astern in your sign convention).
- \(k_{PW}\) [-]: transverse-thrust ratio (tunable).
- \(s\in\{-1,+1\}\): sign from prop handedness and telegraph direction (defined explicitly in section D).
- \(x_P\) [m]: propeller longitudinal position (aft is negative if \(x\) forward).
- \(x_{ref}\) [m]: reference point about which \(N\) is computed (usually CG).

**Units / dimensional consistency**  
\(Y_P\) has units N; \(N_P\) has units N·m.

**Pros**
- Minimal data: uses \(X_P\), handedness, and \(x_P\).
- Very stable numerically (easy to clamp/smooth).
- Easy to tune from “stern kick” phenomenology.

**Cons**
- Too strong at higher sternway unless you add decay.
- Cannot represent the empirically observed dependence on advance ratio / evolving inflow during stopping. Practical guidance indicates transverse thrust is “slight” initially when a ship still has headway but grows as headway decays under astern power. citeturn44view0

**Expected behavior**
- Ahead: if enabled, creates a small steady bias; but you likely want it disabled to preserve validated ahead.
- Astern: produces immediate yaw / lateral shove.

#### Candidate model: thrust-proportional with a decay envelope in \(|J|\) (recommended class)

**Formula**
\[
Y_P = k_{PW}\,f(|J|)\,|X_P|\,s,\qquad N_P=(x_P-x_{ref})\,Y_P
\]
with a smooth envelope such as:
\[
f(|J|)=\frac{1}{1+\left(\frac{|J|}{J_{0}}\right)^p}
\]

**Definitions**
- \(J = \dfrac{u_A}{nD}\) (advance ratio); use \(|J|\) to avoid quadrant sign confusion.
- \(u_A\) [m/s]: inflow speed at propeller plane (could be \(u(1-w_P)\) if you already compute wake). Standard MMG uses \(J_P=u(1-w_P)/(n_PD_P)\). citeturn10view2turn41view0
- \(n\) [rps], \(D\) [m]: shaft speed and propeller diameter (if available).
- \(J_0\) [-]: decay scale; \(p\) [-]: roll-off sharpness.

**Pros**
- Matches empirical/operational observation: strong near bollard pull / low \(|J|\), weaker as sternway becomes established.
- Scale-invariant across ships (non-dimensional).
- Lets you keep ahead behavior unchanged by setting \(k_{PW,ahead}=0\).

**Cons**
- Needs \(n\) and \(D\) for true \(J\) (though can be approximated; see section E).
- Still a “net-effect” model; doesn’t explicitly model distributed hull forces seen in reversing experiments. citeturn4view0turn4view1turn6view0

**Expected behavior**
- Astern stopping: when \(n<0\) and \(u>0\), \(|J|\) starts larger and decreases as \(u\to0\), so prop-walk ramps up—consistent with practical guidance. citeturn44view0
- Astern steady: once \(|u|\) increases (sternway), \(|J|\) increases and the effect decays.

#### Candidate model: propeller side-force coefficient \(K_Y(J)\) (maneuvering-hydrodynamics style)

A common dimensional structure is:
\[
Y_P = \rho D^4 n^2 K_Y(J)
\]
which parallels thrust and torque expressions; some references also discuss semi-empirical relations tying \(Y\) to transverse inflow and torque coefficient. citeturn40view0

**Pros**
- Hydrodynamically conventional form (nice scaling).
- Naturally tied to four-quadrant propeller characterization if you have it.

**Cons**
- Requires a usable \(K_Y(J)\) fit in relevant quadrants (especially reversing), which you likely do not have for “limited vessel data.”
- If you don’t already model four-quadrant \(K_T,J\) robustly, this can become a larger subsystem than you want.

**Expected behavior**
- Potentially good across a wider envelope if coefficients exist; otherwise risky/guessy.

#### Candidate model: four-quadrant inflow-angle model with \(C_{PY}(\theta_P), C_{PN}(\theta_P)\)

A review of berthing maneuvering models summarizes a four-quadrant approach in which:
\[
Y_P = \frac{\rho}{2}S_P V_r^2 C_{PY}(\theta_P),\qquad
N_P = \frac{\rho}{2}S_P V_r^2 C_{PN}(\theta_P)
\]
with \(\theta_P=\tan^{-1}\!\left(\dfrac{V_A}{0.7\pi n D}\right)\) and \(V_r=\sqrt{V_A^2+(0.7\pi n D)^2}\). citeturn43view0

**Pros**
- Explicitly admits **independent \(Y_P\) and \(N_P\)**, useful when net yaw is not well represented by a single force at \(x_P\).
- Conceptually consistent with “off-design / four-quadrant” berthing conditions. citeturn43view0

**Cons**
- Needs coefficient functions \(C_{PY}(\theta_P)\), \(C_{PN}(\theta_P)\) that are not generally available without tests/identification.
- Higher tuning burden.

**Expected behavior**
- Can represent nuanced transitions between quadrants if coefficients are credible.

### D. Recommended implementation model

#### What to compute

Compute **\(Y_P\) as a function of existing propeller surge force** and then compute **\(N_P\)** primarily from the lever arm:
\[
N_P = (x_P-x_{ref})\,Y_P
\]
Add an **optional correction moment** term only if you later find the “stern kick” yaw magnitude cannot be matched cleanly.

This choice is motivated by:
- MMG modularity: baseline MMG already produces \(X_P\) from thrust and thrust deduction. citeturn10view2turn41view0
- Practical shiphandling: transverse thrust is strongest astern and manifests as a stern side-force that creates yaw through its aft lever. citeturn44view0
- Reversing experiments: the net effect is often a force system acting mainly on the aft body with a significant moment arm; representing it as an equivalent force near the propeller is a pragmatic reduction. citeturn4view0turn4view1turn6view0

#### Exact force formula to implement first

Use the **\(|J|\)-decayed thrust-proportional** model, with a safe fallback if \(J\) is unavailable.

**Primary (preferred) form**
\[
Y_P = k_{PW,gear}\; f(|J|)\; |X_P|\; s
\]
where
\[
f(|J|)=\frac{1}{1+\left(\frac{|J|}{J_0}\right)^p}
\]

**Fallback (if you cannot compute \(J\))**
\[
Y_P = k_{PW,gear}\; f_u(|u|)\; |X_P|\; s,\qquad
f_u(|u|)=\frac{1}{1+\left(\frac{|u|}{u_0}\right)^p}
\]

**Yaw moment**
\[
N_P = (x_P-x_{ref})\,Y_P \;+\; k_{NP}\,f(|J|)\,|X_P|\,L
\]
- Set \(k_{NP}=0\) initially.

#### Recommended sign logic (unambiguous)

Adopt the coordinate convention used in standard MMG documentation unless your engine already differs:

- \(+x\): forward (toward bow)  
- \(+y\): starboard  
- \(+N\): starboard yaw (clockwise, bow to starboard) citeturn38view1turn39view0

Define:

- **Right-handed propeller (RH)**: rotates **clockwise when viewed from astern while producing ahead thrust** (common shiphandling definition). citeturn44view0  
- Let \(hand = +1\) for RH, \(hand=-1\) for LH.
- Let \(gear = \mathrm{sign}(n_{cmd})\) where:
  - \(gear=+1\): ahead telegraph (shaft direction that produces ahead thrust)
  - \(gear=-1\): astern telegraph

Then use:
\[
s = hand \cdot gear
\]

This matches shiphandling direction described for RH props:
- RH + **ahead**: slight tendency for bow to swing to port (stern to starboard) → \(Y_P>0\) if you enable ahead bias. citeturn44view0
- RH + **astern**: stern pushed to port (bow to starboard) → \(Y_P<0\) and, with \(x_P< x_{ref}\), \(N_P>0\). citeturn44view0turn6view0

If your engine instead uses \(+y\) to **port**, flip the sign: \(Y_P \leftarrow -Y_P\) (and \(N_P\) follows from your moment convention).

#### Notes on clamping, smoothing, and stability

Prop-walk can be “impulsive” in a discrete-time integrator if you switch it on/off abruptly. Use these measures:

- **RPM/command deadband**: if \(|n_{cmd}|<n_{dead}\), set \(Y_P=0\) to prevent jitter.
- **Smooth gear sign**: use a smoothed sign function (e.g., \(\tanh(n/n_s)\)) so that crossing neutral doesn’t create a discontinuity.
- **Clamp**: limit \(|Y_P|\le k_{PW,max}\,|X_P|\). Reversing experiments show aft lateral force normalized by thrust can reach on the order of a few tenths in some cases, so a conservative default clamp like \(k_{PW,max}\in[0.25,0.35]\) keeps the model in a plausible regime while still allowing strong stern kick. citeturn4view0turn4view1
- **Optional low-speed-only by default**: to protect the validated ahead regime, set \(k_{PW,ahead}=0\) and only enable astern coefficient, consistent with model-documentation practice that often neglects \(Y_P,N_P\) in baseline MMG contexts. citeturn41view0turn10view2

### E. Calibration guidance

#### Minimum required parameters to add to your vessel schema

1. `propHandedness`: `"RH"` or `"LH"`  
2. `kPW_ast`: transverse thrust ratio in astern at very low \(|J|\) (dimensionless)  
3. `xP`: prop longitudinal position (you already have)  
4. *(If you want \(|J|\)-decay)* `J0` and `p` (or keep `p` global and only store `J0`)

#### Nice-to-have parameters

- `kPW_ahead` (default 0): small ahead bias if you later want it
- `kNP` (default 0): extra yaw-moment ratio if lever-arm-only underestimates yaw
- `kPW_max` clamp ratio (default ~0.30)
- `u0` (fallback scale if you can’t compute \(J\))

#### Typical ranges and defensible defaults

Because “prop-walk” is strongly hull/prop/shaft-depth dependent, literature rarely gives one universal coefficient. The most defensible approach is to set **broad but bounded defaults** and then tune with simple maneuvers.

A pragmatic default set for single-screw monohulls aimed at docking realism:

- \(k_{PW,ast}\): **0.08–0.18**, default **0.12**  
  - Justification: reversing experiments show stern lateral forces on the order of **~0.15–0.30 of thrust** in some conditions, with opposing bow contributions, implying a net “equivalent single-force” ratio often in the **~0.05–0.20** range depending on hull and how you lump forces into the propeller module. citeturn4view0turn4view1
  - Practical shiphandling guidance emphasizes astern dominance but not a single magnitude, consistent with needing tuning. citeturn44view0

- \(J_0\): **0.2–0.5**, default **0.35** (dimensionless)  
  - Interpretation: around \(|J|\sim J_0\), the effect is about half-strength. This captures the shiphandling observation that transverse thrust ramps up as headway decays under astern power and decays as sternway becomes established. citeturn44view0

- \(p\): **1.5–3**, default **2** (global constant is fine)

- \(k_{PW,max}\): **0.25–0.35**, default **0.30** (clamp)

- \(k_{PW,ahead}\): default **0.0** (to avoid disturbing validated ahead)

#### Estimating coefficients with limited vessel data

If you only know “single-screw monohull + RH/LH + approximate size,” use a staged estimation:

1. **Start with a conservative astern coefficient**: \(k_{PW,ast}=0.10\), \(J_0=0.35\), \(p=2\).  
2. **Set the sign from handedness** using the shiphandling rule: for RH, astern tends to push stern to port (bow to starboard). citeturn44view0  
3. **Tune \(k_{PW,ast}\) to match qualitative realism first**, not exact numbers:
   - If your stern “kicks” unrealistically hard on small astern input, reduce \(k_{PW,ast}\) or tighten clamp.
   - If the vessel backs “too straight” and feels dead in docking, increase it.
4. **Tune decay \(J_0\)** by watching behavior as sternway builds:
   - If the ship keeps walking sideways even at a steady astern speed, lower \(J_0\) (faster decay) or raise \(p\).
   - If the stern kick disappears too quickly once moving, raise \(J_0\).

#### Tuning from observed skipper behavior or simple trials

Even without instrumented trials, you can tune from “operator expectations” consistent with guidance:

- When shifting to astern while still moving ahead, transverse thrust is initially slight but grows as forward speed drops. citeturn44view0  
So, if your simulation immediately yaws hard the moment astern is applied at 2–3 kn ahead speed, your decay function is too weak (need smaller effect at larger \(|J|\)/\(|u|\)).

- Rudder effectiveness tends to degrade when engines are put astern (prop flow to rudder changes/breaks), while transverse thrust becomes disruptive. citeturn44view0turn6view0  
So, for docking feel, it is often acceptable that **yaw is dominated by prop-walk at very low speeds in reverse**, unless you have detailed reverse-rudder models.

### F. Validation plan

Your goal is “plausible close-quarters behavior” with deterministic physics. These scenarios validate both sign and scaling.

#### Scenario: zero rudder, vessel initially stopped, apply astern RPM

**Setup**: \(u=v=r=0\), \(\delta=0\). Command a fixed astern telegraph (negative \(n\) or negative \(X_P\)).

**Expected qualitative behavior**
- The ship should develop **immediate yaw** and a lateral acceleration consistent with a stern shove.
- For **RH** prop: output should correspond to **stern to port, bow to starboard**. citeturn44view0turn6view0  
- For **LH**: the opposite.

**Expected quantitative checks**
- At very low speed (small \(|J|\)), \(|Y_P|/|X_P|\) should be close to \(k_{PW,ast}\) (subject to clamp).
- Doubling RPM (if your \(X_P\sim n^2\)) should approximately quadruple initial yaw acceleration (before hull crossflow terms dominate).

**Unit tests**
- Sign test: RH+astern ⇒ \(Y_P<0\) (if \(+y\)=starboard), \(N_P>0\) (if \(x_P<x_{ref}\)).  
- Continuity test: ramp in \(n\) produces continuous \(Y_P\) (no step).

#### Scenario: zero rudder, already moving astern, then apply additional astern power

**Setup**: start with \(u<0\) (sternway), then step to stronger astern.

**Expected**
- Prop-walk should be **weaker than the “from stop” case** at the same command if \(|J|\) is larger, reflecting decay.
- Ship should not continue to “crab” excessively at high sternway.

This is your primary guardrail against runaway sideways motion.

#### Scenario: ahead propulsion at low speed

**Setup**: \(u\approx 0\), command small ahead, \(\delta=0\).

**Expected**
- With default \(k_{PW,ahead}=0\), results should match your current validated behavior exactly (bitwise, if deterministic).

This is how you guarantee you did not regress ahead behavior.

#### Scenario: reverse propulsion with different RPM magnitudes

**Expected**
- At very low speed, yaw/lateral acceleration should scale with \(|X_P|\).  
- At higher \(|u|\), the decay envelope should dominate.

#### Scenario: compare RH vs LH prop

Run identical conditions with handedness flipped.

**Expected**
- \(Y_P\) and \(N_P\) signs must invert cleanly; magnitudes should match.

#### Scenario: interaction with yaw moment and “stern kick”

Compare:
- Lever-only moment \(N_P=(x_P-x_{ref})Y_P\)
- Lever-only plus correction \(k_{NP}\neq 0\)

**Expected**
- If you later use \(k_{NP}\), it should mainly affect **yaw rate development** without introducing huge lateral drift.
- Use reversing experimental evidence as a plausibility anchor: reversing can induce significant lateral forces and moments normalized by thrust and ship length. citeturn4view0turn4view1

#### Deterministic engine test ideas

- **Property-based tests**:
  - \(|Y_P|\) monotone increasing with \(|X_P|\) (all else equal).
  - \(|Y_P|\) monotone decreasing with \(|J|\) or \(|u|\) (all else equal).
- **Regression fixtures**:
  - Keep a snapshot of ahead-turning/zigzag outputs; ensure unchanged.
- **Time-step stability test**:
  - Run the same maneuver at \(\Delta t\) and \(\Delta t/2\); confirm qualitative similarity and bounded error.

### G. Implementation-oriented pseudocode

```ts
// Prop-walk / transverse thrust add-on for an MMG-style 3DOF model.
// Assumes body axes: +x forward, +y starboard, +yaw moment N turns bow to starboard.
// If your sim uses +y to port, flip the sign of Y_P (or flip handednessSign).

type PropHandedness = "RH" | "LH";

interface VesselParams {
  // geometry
  xRef: number;      // [m] reference x about which yaw moment is computed (often CG x)
  xP: number;        // [m] propeller x position in body frame (aft negative if origin near midships/CG)
  L: number;         // [m] characteristic length (Lpp or LOA) for optional moment correction

  // prop-walk coefficients
  handedness: PropHandedness;
  kPW_ast: number;   // [-] transverse thrust ratio in astern at low |J|
  kPW_ahead: number; // [-] default 0 to preserve validated ahead behavior
  kPW_max: number;   // [-] clamp, e.g. 0.30

  // decay envelope (choose either J-based or u-based)
  J0?: number;       // [-] if you can compute J
  u0?: number;       // [m/s] fallback if J not available
  pDecay: number;    // [-] e.g. 2.0

  // optional additional moment correction (start at 0)
  kNP: number;       // [-] (scaled by |X_P| * L)
}

interface State {
  u: number; // [m/s] surge velocity in body axes (+ahead)
  v: number; // [m/s] sway velocity (+starboard)
  r: number; // [rad/s] yaw rate (+turning to starboard)
}

interface PropInput {
  nCmd: number;  // [rps] commanded shaft rate; sign defines telegraph (+ahead, -astern)
  D?: number;    // [m] propeller diameter if available
  // If you already compute effective prop inflow uA or wake fraction, you can pass that too.
}

function smoothSign(x: number, eps: number): number {
  // Continuous approximation of sign(x) in [-1, +1]
  return Math.tanh(x / eps);
}

function clamp(x: number, lo: number, hi: number): number {
  return Math.max(lo, Math.min(hi, x));
}

function decay1over1plus(x: number, x0: number, p: number): number {
  // f = 1 / (1 + (x/x0)^p)
  if (x0 <= 0) return 0;
  const y = Math.pow(Math.abs(x) / x0, p);
  return 1 / (1 + y);
}

function computePropWalkForces(
  state: State,
  X_P: number,          // [N] existing propeller surge force (signed)
  params: VesselParams,
  prop: PropInput
): { Y_P: number; N_P: number } {
  // 1) Deadband & smooth telegraph sign
  const nDead = 0.05;   // [rps] tweak as needed
  const nSignEps = 0.10; // [rps] smoothing scale

  if (Math.abs(prop.nCmd) < nDead) {
    return { Y_P: 0, N_P: 0 };
  }

  const gear = smoothSign(prop.nCmd, nSignEps); // ~ +1 ahead, ~ -1 astern

  // 2) Handedness sign: RH = +1, LH = -1 (definition: RH rotates CW viewed from astern in ahead)
  const hand = (params.handedness === "RH") ? 1 : -1;

  // 3) Choose coefficient by telegraph
  // By default protect ahead: kPW_ahead = 0
  const kPW = (gear < 0) ? params.kPW_ast : params.kPW_ahead;

  if (kPW === 0) {
    return { Y_P: 0, N_P: 0 };
  }

  // 4) Decay envelope: prefer J-based if D and J0 exist, else u-based
  let f = 1.0;
  const absU = Math.abs(state.u);

  if (params.J0 !== undefined && prop.D !== undefined) {
    const nAbs = Math.max(Math.abs(prop.nCmd), nDead);
    const Jabs = absU / (nAbs * prop.D); // approximate |J| (use uA if you have it)
    f = decay1over1plus(Jabs, params.J0, params.pDecay);
  } else if (params.u0 !== undefined) {
    f = decay1over1plus(absU, params.u0, params.pDecay);
  }

  // 5) Core transverse thrust model (thrust-proportional), with clamp
  const base = kPW * f * Math.abs(X_P);
  const Y_unclamped = base * hand * gear; // sign logic
  const Y_lim = params.kPW_max * Math.abs(X_P);
  const Y_P = clamp(Y_unclamped, -Y_lim, +Y_lim);

  // 6) Yaw moment from lever arm + optional correction
  const lever = (params.xP - params.xRef); // [m]
  let N_P = lever * Y_P;

  if (params.kNP !== 0) {
    N_P += params.kNP * f * Math.abs(X_P) * params.L * hand * gear;
  }

  return { Y_P, N_P };
}

// Slot into MMG force balance each step:
// FX = X_H + X_R + X_P + ... (no change)
// FY = Y_H + Y_R + Y_P + ...
// N  = N_H + N_R + N_P + ...
```

## Prop-Walk Implementation for MMG-Style 3DOF Maneuvering Model

### A. Executive Recommendation

**Recommended model: Single-coefficient empirical transverse propeller force `Y_P` applied at the propeller's longitudinal position `x_P`, with yaw moment derived as `N_P = x_P · Y_P`.**

The formula:

```
Y_P = σ_h · C_PY · ρ · n_P · |n_P| · D_P⁴ · f(J_P)
```

where `σ_h` encodes propeller handedness (±1), `C_PY` is a single tunable transverse-force coefficient, and `f(J_P)` is a blending function that amplifies the effect at low advance ratios (low speed / high RPM) and suppresses it at high advance ratios (forward cruising).

**Why this model:**

1. **Minimal parameter burden** — one new primary coefficient `C_PY` plus handedness sign `σ_h`, both easily defaultable.
2. **Physically grounded** — propeller walk is caused by the water moved by the propeller in an axial direction and in a rotation; if the rotating water cone contacts the ship's hull, a sideways force is generated. When in reverse gear, the water will hit the hull directly, resulting in propeller walk.
3. **Clean MMG integration** — the MMG model decomposes forces as `N = N_H + N_P + N_R + N_A`, and this adds `Y_P` and `N_P` terms without modifying any existing hull or rudder modules.
4. **Ahead behavior preserved** — the speed-dependent blending function `f(J_P)` naturally suppresses the transverse force at normal forward speeds, matching the well-known observation that propeller walk is hardly noticeable when sailing forward, since the propeller water will not hit a large surface of the ship's hull and corrections to the ship's course can easily be made with the rudder.
5. **Astern behavior improved** — this is where prop-walk matters most. In reverse gear, the turning effect will be much stronger and with opposite direction.

### B. Source-Backed Theory

#### B.1 Physical Causes of Prop-Walk

Prop-walk arises from several interacting physical mechanisms:

**1. Pressure gradient asymmetry (primary mechanism):**
A propeller doesn't spin in a perfectly uniform environment. Water pressure increases with depth. This means the lower blades of the propeller are pushing against denser, more resistant water than the upper blades. This imbalance creates a net sideways force. The descending blade (on the side where the blade moves downward) encounters higher pressure and produces greater thrust; the ascending blade operates at lower pressure. The net asymmetry produces a transverse force component.

**2. Helical slipstream / hull interaction (dominant in astern):**
Propeller walk is caused by the water moved by the propeller in an axial direction and in a rotation. The water, coming from the propeller, gets a cone shape, widening when it leaves the propeller. If the rotating water cone contacts the ship's hull, a sideways force is generated. When going astern, the propeller discharge (slipstream) is directed forward onto the hull's afterbody and deadwood, creating a large lateral force. When going ahead, the slipstream is directed aft away from the hull.

**3. Paddle-wheel analogy:**
The "paddle wheel effect" states that when the vessel is stationary and the propeller started, the propeller will move the afterbody of the ship in the direction of rotation: that is in the sense of a paddle or road wheel moving relative to the ground.

#### B.2 Directional Behavior

For a **right-handed propeller** (clockwise when viewed from astern in forward gear):

- A right-handed propeller in forward gear will tend to push the stern of the boat to starboard (thereby pushing the bow to port and turning the boat counter-clockwise).
- In reverse gear, the turning effect will be much stronger and with opposite direction (pushing the aft to port).
- A left-handed propeller acts analogically to the right-handed but with all rotation directions reversed.

#### B.3 Speed Dependence

When a propeller is put astern on a ship moving forward at speed, the initial effect of transverse thrust is slight. However, as the ship's forward motion decreases, the effect of transverse thrust increases. At higher speeds, the rudder becomes much more effective, and the transverse thrust is usually negligible.

#### B.4 Magnitude

The magnitude of the transverse thrust when going astern is in the order of 10-14% of the total thrust on the propeller. This is a well-known industry heuristic from seamanship training materials. Some sources cite lower values (5–8%) for ahead operation.

#### B.5 How the Literature Models It

The standard MMG method (Yasukawa & Yoshimura, 2015) has checked the details of existing MMG models such as the coordinate system, the motion equations, the hull and rudder hydrodynamic force models etc., in view of accuracy, simplicity, physical/theoretical background. However, the standard MMG method focuses primarily on first-quadrant (ahead) operation and does not explicitly include a prop-walk `Y_P` term.

A practical MMG ship mathematic model with propeller reversing transverse forces and low speed correction has been discussed for application to the right-handed single-screw ship in the berthing control literature (Jia & Yang, 2016, *Int. J. Naval Arch. Ocean Eng.*).

Sutulo and Guedes Soares developed an extension for low-speed manoeuvres including: 4-quadrant model for propeller thrust, model for the propeller-generated sway force and yaw moment, 4-quadrant model for the rudder forces including the slipstream influence.

Recent work proposes improved maneuvering motion models incorporating hydrodynamic forces associated with low-speed maneuvering, including large drift-angle motions from side thruster operation and astern movements due to propeller reversal.

### C. Candidate Formulas

#### C.1 Model A: Thrust-Fraction Model (Simplest)

**Formula:**
```
Y_P = σ_h · C_PY · X_P_thrust
N_P = x_P · Y_P
```

where `X_P_thrust = (1 - t_P) · T` is the effective propeller surge force already computed.

**Variable definitions:**
- `σ_h`: handedness sign (+1 for RH prop, −1 for LH)
- `C_PY`: fraction of axial thrust appearing as transverse force (dimensionless)
- `T`: propeller thrust [N]
- `t_P`: thrust deduction factor
- `x_P`: propeller x-position relative to midship [m] (negative, since propeller is aft)

**Sign conventions:**
- Y positive to starboard (standard MMG body frame)
- For RH prop going ahead: stern kicks starboard → `Y_P` positive
- For RH prop going astern: stern kicks port → `Y_P` negative (since thrust reverses)

**Pros:**
- Extremely simple — one coefficient
- Automatically scales with engine power
- Dimensionally consistent

**Cons:**
- No speed dependence: effect is the same at 0 knots and 15 knots, which is physically wrong
- Over-predicts effect at high forward speeds
- Under-predicts the astern/ahead asymmetry (astern effect is typically 2–3× stronger)

**Data required:** Only `C_PY`

**Typical `C_PY` range:** 0.04–0.08 ahead, 0.10–0.15 astern (from the ~10–14% heuristic)

**Verdict:** Too simple. Fails the requirement of not disturbing ahead behavior.

---

#### C.2 Model B: Advance-Ratio-Dependent Model (Recommended)

**Formula:**
```
Y_P = σ_h · C_PY · ρ · |n_P| · n_P · D_P⁴ · f(J_P)
N_P = x_P · Y_P
```

**Variable definitions:**
- `σ_h`: handedness sign (+1 for RH, −1 for LH)
- `C_PY`: transverse force coefficient (dimensionless, analogous to `K_T`)
- `ρ`: water density [kg/m³]
- `n_P`: propeller revolutions per second [rps] (positive = ahead rotation)
- `D_P`: propeller diameter [m]
- `J_P`: advance ratio = `u_P / (n_P · D_P)` where `u_P = u · (1 - w_P)` is the advance velocity at the propeller
- `f(J_P)`: blending function that modulates with speed

**Blending function `f(J_P)`:**
```
f(J_P) = 1 / (1 + (J_P / J_ref)²)
```

This is a smooth Lorentzian-style rolloff. At `J_P = 0` (bollard pull), `f = 1` (full effect). At `J_P = J_ref`, `f = 0.5`. At high `J`, `f → 0`.

For astern operation (n_P < 0), the advance ratio becomes negative or uses the four-quadrant angle. The key insight: when the propeller is reversed, the slipstream hits the hull, amplifying the effect. This is handled by using different `C_PY` multipliers for ahead vs astern:

```
C_PY_effective = (n_P > 0) ? C_PY_ahead : C_PY_astern
```

with typical ratio `C_PY_astern / C_PY_ahead ≈ 2.0 – 3.0`.

**Dimensional analysis:** `ρ · n_P · |n_P| · D_P⁴` has units [kg/m³ · s⁻² · m⁴] = [N], correct for force.

**Sign logic for RH prop (`σ_h = +1`):**
- Ahead (`n_P > 0`): `|n_P| · n_P > 0`, so `Y_P > 0` → stern to starboard ✓
- Astern (`n_P < 0`): `|n_P| · n_P < 0`, so `Y_P < 0` → stern to port ✓

**Pros:**
- Physically motivated speed dependence
- Clean ahead/astern asymmetry
- Uses the same `ρ · n² · D⁴` scaling as standard propeller thrust
- Only 3 tunable parameters (`C_PY_ahead`, `C_PY_astern`, `J_ref`)
- Near-zero effect at cruise speed

**Cons:**
- Need to handle `J_P` singularity when `n_P → 0` (but trivially handled: when `n_P ≈ 0`, force vanishes anyway via the `n² `term)
- Blending function shape is an engineering approximation

**Data required:** `C_PY_ahead`, `C_PY_astern`, `J_ref`, plus existing vessel parameters (`D_P`, `ρ`, `w_P`, `x_P`)

---

#### C.3 Model C: Independent Y_P and N_P with Four-Quadrant Angle (High-Fidelity)

**Formula:**
```
φ_P = atan2(u_P, 0.7π · n_P · D_P)

Y_P = ρ · D_P² · V_R² · C_PY(φ_P)
N_P = ρ · D_P² · L_PP · V_R² · C_PN(φ_P)
```

where `V_R = sqrt(u_P² + (0.7π · n_P · D_P)²)` is the resultant inflow velocity at 70% blade radius, and `C_PY(φ_P)`, `C_PN(φ_P)` are tabulated or Fourier-series functions of the four-quadrant angle.

This approach follows Okuda et al. (2023), who modeled `C_PY(φ_P)` and `C_PN(φ_P)` as functions of the four-quadrant propeller angle, and included both `Y_P` and `N_P` propeller contributions.

**Pros:**
- Most physically accurate
- Handles all four quadrants smoothly
- Independent `Y_P` and `N_P` allow for non-trivial moment behavior

**Cons:**
- Requires tabulated or Fourier-fit data for `C_PY(φ_P)` and `C_PN(φ_P)` — typically from captive model tests
- Significantly more complex to implement
- Vessel-specific data rarely available without dedicated experiments

**Data required:** Fourier coefficients or tabulated `C_PY`, `C_PN` vs `φ_P` — **high-fidelity optional, requires CFD or model tests**

**Verdict:** Ideal for research-grade simulators with per-vessel data. Too data-hungry for your use case.

---

#### C.4 Model D: Torque-Reaction Model

**Formula:**
```
Y_P = σ_h · C_TQ · Q_P / (0.5 · D_P)
N_P = x_P · Y_P
```

where `Q_P` is the propeller torque.

**Rationale:** The torque reaction creates a rolling moment; in combination with the hull's asymmetric underwater geometry, this translates into a sway force.

**Pros:** Physically motivated for the rolling component of prop-walk.

**Cons:** Requires a torque model (additional `K_Q` data). In a 3DOF model without roll, the mechanism is indirect. Less relevant than slipstream/hull interaction for the lateral force.

**Verdict:** Adds complexity for marginal benefit in a 3DOF framework. Not recommended as primary model.

### D. Recommended Implementation Model

#### D.1 Exact Formulas

Implement Model B with ahead/astern split:

```
// ——— Inputs ———
// n_P     : propeller rps (positive = ahead rotation for this prop)
// u       : vessel surge velocity [m/s] (positive = ahead motion)
// w_P     : wake fraction at propeller (typically 0.2–0.4)
// D_P     : propeller diameter [m]
// ρ       : water density [kg/m³]
// x_P     : longitudinal position of propeller from midship [m] (negative, since aft)
// σ_h     : handedness sign: +1 for RH prop, −1 for LH prop
// C_PY_ahead  : transverse force coefficient, ahead operation
// C_PY_astern : transverse force coefficient, astern operation
// J_ref   : advance ratio reference for speed blending

// ——— Step 1: Advance velocity and ratio ———
u_P = u * (1 - w_P)                    // advance velocity at propeller

// Guard against division by zero:
if |n_P| < n_P_min:
    Y_P = 0
    N_P = 0
    return

J_P = u_P / (n_P * D_P)               // signed advance ratio

// ——— Step 2: Speed blending function ———
f_J = 1.0 / (1.0 + (J_P / J_ref)²)

// ——— Step 3: Ahead/astern coefficient selection ———
if n_P > 0:
    C_PY = C_PY_ahead
else:
    C_PY = C_PY_astern

// ——— Step 4: Transverse force ———
Y_P = σ_h * C_PY * ρ * |n_P| * n_P * D_P⁴ * f_J

// ——— Step 5: Yaw moment ———
N_P = x_P * Y_P
```

#### D.2 Should N_P Be Derived from Y_P · x_P?

**Yes, for primary implementation.** The physical force is applied at the propeller location, so `N_P = x_P · Y_P` is the correct starting point. An additional empirical correction term `ΔN_P` could be added later if validation shows the yaw moment is systematically over- or under-predicted. For now:

```
N_P = x_P * Y_P    // primary
// N_P = x_P * Y_P + ΔN_P   // optional correction, add only if validation demands
```

#### D.3 Complete Sign Logic Table

| Propeller | σ_h | n_P direction | Y_P sign | Stern moves | Bow swings |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| RH | +1 | Ahead (+) | + | Starboard | Port |
| RH | +1 | Astern (−) | − | Port | Starboard |
| LH | −1 | Ahead (+) | − | Port | Starboard |
| LH | −1 | Astern (−) | + | Starboard | Port |

(Y positive = starboard in standard body frame with x forward, y to starboard)

This matches the well-known seamanship rule: A typical right-handed, or clockwise-rotating propeller, tends to rotate the stern to starboard when moving forwards. As well, the opposite occurs when going astern, as the stern tends to rotate to port.

#### D.4 Numerical Stability Notes

1. **Deadband on `n_P`**: Use `n_P_min = 0.01 rps` (or smaller). When `|n_P| < n_P_min`, set `Y_P = N_P = 0`. This avoids division by zero in `J_P` and produces negligible error since the `n²` term drives force to zero anyway.

2. **Clamping `f_J`**: The Lorentzian is naturally bounded in [0, 1]. No clamping needed.

3. **Smooth transition at `n_P = 0` crossing**: The `|n_P| · n_P` term and coefficient switching at `n_P = 0` can cause a discontinuity in coefficient value. Use a smooth blend:
```
α = 0.5 * (1 + tanh(n_P / n_P_blend))  // smooth 0→1 transition
C_PY = α * C_PY_ahead + (1 - α) * C_PY_astern
```
where `n_P_blend ≈ 0.1–0.5 rps` controls the transition width.

4. **Wake fraction in astern**: The ahead-motion `w_P` is not physically correct for astern operation (the wake structure changes completely). A pragmatic fix: use `w_P_astern ≈ 0` or a reduced value when `n_P < 0`. This also affects your existing `X_P` model and should be considered there as well.

#### E.3 Typical Coefficient Ranges

The magnitude of the transverse thrust when going astern is in the order of 10-14% of the total thrust on the propeller.

Working backward from this:
- At bollard pull (J=0): `Y_P / T = C_PY / K_T`
- With `K_T(J=0) ≈ 0.35–0.50` (typical propeller):
- If `Y_P/T ≈ 0.10–0.14`, then `C_PY_astern ≈ 0.035–0.070`

**Recommended defaults:**

| Parameter | Default | Range | Evidence level |
|---|---|---|---|
| `C_PY_astern` | 0.04 | 0.02–0.08 | Industry heuristic (10–14% of thrust) |
| `C_PY_ahead` | 0.015 | 0.005–0.03 | Common observation: ahead effect ~2–3× weaker |
| `J_ref` | 0.4 | 0.2–0.6 | Engineering approximation |

#### E.4 Estimation Without Vessel-Specific Data

If only vessel type and propeller diameter are known:

1. **Use defaults above** — they produce qualitatively correct behavior for conventional single-screw merchant vessels.
2. **Scale by prop diameter relative to draft**: deeper draft with same prop = slightly stronger effect (more asymmetry between upper and lower blade paths). Adjust `C_PY` by ±20% based on `D_P / d` ratio.
3. **Hull fullness**: fuller hulls (high `C_b`) have larger afterbody area for slipstream interaction → slightly higher `C_PY_astern`. Vessels with `C_b > 0.80` might use `C_PY_astern ≈ 0.05–0.07`; slender vessels (`C_b < 0.60`) might use `0.02–0.04`.
4. **Number of blades**: more blades = slightly smoother effect, but magnitude is similar.

#### E.5 Tuning From Observed Behavior

The easiest tuning scenario:

1. **Bollard pull astern test (real or video)**: Start vessel at rest, apply full astern RPM, zero rudder. Observe rate of heading change. Match yaw rate in simulator by adjusting `C_PY_astern`.
2. **Docking approach (real)**: Observe how much rudder correction is needed to hold course during astern approach. If simulation needs less rudder than reality, increase `C_PY_astern`.
3. **Ahead course offset**: If vessel shows a slight port tendency (RH prop) at low speed ahead, tune `C_PY_ahead`.
4. **Speed-dependent fade**: If the effect persists too long at higher speeds, reduce `J_ref`. If it vanishes too soon, increase `J_ref`.

### F. Validation Plan

#### F.1 Scenario: Zero rudder, vessel initially stopped, astern RPM applied

**Expected behavior (RH prop):**
- Vessel begins to make sternway
- Stern walks to port (Y_P < 0, since n_P < 0)
- Bow swings to starboard
- Heading change rate increases with RPM
- Heading change rate is maximum at low speed (just after engine start) and reduces as sternway builds and `|J_P|` increases

**Quantitative check:** For a ~170m vessel at full astern RPM, initial yaw rate should develop to roughly 0.5–2.0 deg/s within 30–60 seconds (order of magnitude; vessel-dependent).

#### F.2 Scenario: Zero rudder, already moving astern at steady speed

**Expected behavior:**
- Transverse force still present but reduced (higher `|J_P|` means `f_J` is lower)
- Vessel may show slight port-ward drift (RH prop astern)
- Rudder becomes more effective (due to sternway through water), so course can be held with moderate helm

#### F.3 Scenario: Ahead propulsion at low speed

**Expected behavior (RH prop):**
- Slight tendency for stern to starboard, bow to port
- The effect of transverse thrust is a slight tendency for the bow to swing to port on a ship with a right-handed propeller turning ahead.
- Effect easily corrected by rudder
- Effect should be barely noticeable above ~5 knots

**Quantitative check:** The ahead transverse force should produce no more than ~0.2–0.5 deg/s yaw rate at dead slow ahead with zero rudder.

#### F.4 Scenario: Reverse propulsion with different RPM magnitudes

**Expected behavior:**
- Prop-walk force scales with `n_P²` (quadratic in RPM)
- Doubling RPM ≈ 4× the transverse force
- At very low RPM astern, effect is small
- At full astern RPM, effect dominates steering

#### F.5 Scenario: RH vs LH prop comparison

**Expected behavior:**
- Exact mirror image of all lateral forces and yaw moments
- LH prop: stern to port going ahead, stern to starboard going astern
- Trajectory plots should be mirror-symmetric about centerline

#### F.6 Scenario: Interaction with rudder

**Expected behavior (RH prop, astern operation):**
- With rudder hard to port: rudder force opposes prop-walk → vessel may hold course or turn slightly
- With rudder hard to starboard: rudder force adds to prop-walk → rapid turn to starboard
- Asymmetrical blade thrust occurs in forward and reverse, in opposite directions. However, in forward gear, the rudder easily compensates for it, so you hardly notice it. In reverse gear, the rudder is much less effective, so the blade thrust is more obvious and harder to control.

#### F.7 Unit / Integration Test Ideas

| Test ID | Description | Assertion |
|---|---|---|
| T1 | `n_P = 0` → `Y_P = 0, N_P = 0` | No force when propeller stopped |
| T2 | RH prop, `n_P > 0`, `u = 0` → `Y_P > 0` | Stern starboard at bollard ahead |
| T3 | RH prop, `n_P < 0`, `u = 0` → `Y_P < 0` | Stern port at bollard astern |

### G. Implementation-Oriented Pseudocode

```javascript
/**
 * Compute propeller transverse force (prop-walk) Y_P and yaw moment N_P.
 *
 * Call this alongside your existing propeller thrust computation.
 * The returned Y_P and N_P are added to the sway and yaw force balances.
 *
 * @param {number} n_P        - Propeller rps (positive = ahead rotation)
 * @param {number} u          - Vessel surge velocity [m/s] (positive = ahead)
 * @param {object} vessel     - Vessel parameter object
 * @returns {{ Y_P: number, N_P: number }}
 */
function computePropWalk(n_P, u, vessel) {
  const {
    rho,            // water density [kg/m³], e.g. 1025
    D_P,            // propeller diameter [m]
    x_P,            // propeller x-position from midship [m] (negative = aft)
    w_P0,           // ahead wake fraction, e.g. 0.30
    sigma_h,        // handedness: +1 = RH, -1 = LH
    C_PY_ahead,     // transverse coeff ahead, e.g. 0.015
    C_PY_astern,    // transverse coeff astern, e.g. 0.040
    J_ref,          // advance ratio reference, e.g. 0.4
    n_P_blend,      // smoothing half-width [rps], e.g. 0.2
  } = vessel;

  // --- Deadband: propeller essentially stopped ---
  const N_P_MIN = 0.01; // rps
  if (Math.abs(n_P) < N_P_MIN) {
    return { Y_P: 0, N_P: 0 };
  }

  // --- Wake fraction: use ahead value for n_P > 0, ~0 for astern ---
  // Smooth blend between ahead wake and astern wake (≈0)
  const alpha_w = 0.5 * (1 + Math.tanh(n_P / Math.max(n_P_blend, 0.01)));
  const w_P = alpha_w * w_P0 + (1 - alpha_w) * 0.0;

  // --- Advance velocity and ratio ---
  const u_P = u * (1 - w_P);
  const J_P = u_P / (n_P * D_P); // signed advance ratio

  // --- Speed blending: suppress effect at high |J| ---
  const f_J = 1.0 / (1.0 + (J_P / J_ref) * (J_P / J_ref));

  // --- Smooth ahead/astern coefficient blend ---
  const alpha_c = 0.5 * (1 + Math.tanh(n_P / Math.max(n_P_blend, 0.01)));
  const C_PY = alpha_c * C_PY_ahead + (1 - alpha_c) * C_PY_astern;

  // --- Transverse force ---
  // ρ * |n_P| * n_P * D_P^4 has units [N]
  const Y_P = sigma_h * C_PY * rho * Math.abs(n_P) * n_P
              * Math.pow(D_P, 4) * f_J;

  // --- Yaw moment from force applied at propeller position ---
  const N_P_moment = x_P * Y_P;

  return { Y_P: Y_P, N_P: N_P_moment };
}
```

## Hydrodynamic Modeling and Implementation of Propeller-Induced Transverse Force for Low-Speed Maneuvering in Modular 3DOF Physics Engines

The precision of low-speed vessel maneuvering simulations is fundamentally dependent on the accurate representation of secondary hydrodynamic effects that are often negligible at cruising speeds. Among these, the propeller-induced transverse force, colloquially known as prop-walk or transverse thrust, represents the most significant gap in standard three-degree-of-freedom (3DOF) Maneuvering Modeling Group (MMG) implementations focused on open-water transit. While the longitudinal surge force XP​ drives the vessel, the asymmetric nature of a rotating propeller creates a lateral force YP​ and a corresponding yaw moment NP​ that dictate the vessel's handling characteristics during docking, berthing, and low-speed backing maneuvers. For a single-screw monohull, the absence of these terms in a physics engine leads to a "sterile" simulation where the vessel moves unnaturally straight in reverse, failing to replicate the "stern kick" that mariners rely on for tight-quarters control.  

### Theoretical Architecture of the Modular Maneuvering Model

The MMG framework is a modular approach that decomposes the total hydrodynamic forces and moments acting on a vessel into contributions from the hull, the propeller, and the rudder, while explicitly modeling the interaction effects between these three entities. This modularity is particularly advantageous for browser-based simulators as it allows for the substitution of different propeller types or rudder geometries without necessitating the recalculation of the entire vessel's hydrodynamic derivative set. In the standard 3DOF formulation, the equations of motion are expressed relative to the midship position or the center of gravity, balancing the mass and inertia against the external forces :  

(m+mx​)u˙−(m+my​)vr−xG​mr2\=XH​+XP​+XR​

(m+my​)v˙+(m+mx​)ur+xG​mr˙\=YH​+YP​+YR​

(IzG​+xG2​m+Jz​)r˙+xG​m(v˙+ur)\=NH​+NP​+NR​

In this context, XP​, YP​, and NP​ represent the longitudinal force, transverse force, and yaw moment induced by the propeller, respectively. While most simplified models assume YP​ and NP​ are zero, a realistic simulation must account for the fact that a rotating propeller in proximity to a hull does not act as a purely axial thruster.  

#### The Role of Propeller-Hull-Rudder Interaction

The complexity of prop-walk arises not just from the propeller itself but from its interaction with the adjacent ship hull and the downstream rudder. In forward motion, the propeller's slipstream is pushed aft, often encountering the rudder before dissipating into the wake. This accelerates the flow over the rudder, enhancing its lift and making the vessel highly controllable. Conversely, in reverse motion, the propeller draws water from the stern and projects a rotating, turbulent slipstream forward against the hull's afterbody. This impingement creates a significant asymmetric pressure distribution, which is the primary driver of the "paddle-wheel effect".  

| Component | Interaction Variable | Physical Manifestation |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Hull-Propeller | Thrust Deduction (tP​) | Resistance increase due to low pressure on the stern. |
| Hull-Propeller | Wake Fraction (wP​) | Reduction in effective inflow speed at the propeller disk. |
| Propeller-Rudder | Flow Straightening (γ) | Reduction of effective rudder angle due to slipstream twist. |
| Propeller-Hull | Transverse Thrust (YP​) | Lateral "walk" caused by slipstream impingement and P-factor. |

### Physical Mechanisms of Propeller-Induced Transverse Force

Simulating prop-walk requires a distinction between its two primary physical origins: the geometric "P-factor" and the interaction-based "slipstream impingement".  

#### Asymmetric Blade Loading (The P-Factor)

The P-factor, also known as asymmetric blade thrust, occurs when the propeller's rotational axis is inclined relative to the direction of the water inflow. In standard monohull designs, the propeller shaft is often raked downward by 8 to 14 degrees to accommodate the engine's internal positioning and the hull's rise toward the stern. As the vessel moves forward, the down-sweeping blade (on the starboard side for a right-handed propeller) moves at a steeper effective angle relative to the water than the up-sweeping blade on the port side. This results in a higher thrust component on the starboard side, creating a horizontal force that tends to yaw the bow to port.  

The magnitude of the P-factor is a function of the shaft angle, the propeller's pitch, and the advance ratio (J). While noticeable in forward gear, it is usually overshadowed by the rudder's effectiveness. However, at very low speeds or during initial acceleration, the P-factor provides the initial "tug" that breaks the vessel's symmetry.  

#### Slipstream Impingement and Asymmetric Pressure Distribution

The more powerful component of prop-walk, particularly in reverse, is caused by the rotation of the propeller's slipstream and its subsequent collision with the hull. A right-handed (RH) propeller rotating clockwise in forward gear creates a clockwise-spiraling slipstream (viewed from aft). When the engine is reversed, the propeller rotates counter-clockwise, and the slipstream is projected forward.  

Because the propeller is situated below the hull's center line, the upper half of the rotating slipstream strikes the upward-sloping sections of the stern. For a RH prop in reverse, the upper part of the slipstream is rotating toward the port side. This creates an area of high pressure on the port side of the stern and a relative low-pressure zone on the starboard side as water is sucked away. The resulting transverse force YP​ pushes the stern to port, which causes the bow to swing to starboard. This effect is significantly stronger than the P-factor because the slipstream is acting directly on the high-surface-area afterbody of the ship.  

### Mathematical Formulation for 3DOF Simulations

For a practical engineering implementation in JavaScript, the transverse propeller force must be integrated into the existing MMG force balance as a function of the propeller's rotational speed (n) and the ship's instantaneous state.  

#### The Coefficient-Based Approach

The most robust method for representing YP​ and NP​ in a maneuvering model is the use of non-dimensional coefficients, analogous to the thrust coefficient KT​ :  

YP​\=ρn2DP4​YP∗​

NP​\=ρn2DP5​NP∗​

Where:

*   ρ is the water density.  
    
*   n is the propeller revolutions per second.  
    
*   DP​ is the propeller diameter.  
    
*   YP∗​ and NP∗​ are non-dimensional coefficients that capture the hull-propeller interaction.  
    

These coefficients are not constants; they vary based on the propeller's loading and the vessel's maneuver state. In a low-speed simulation, the advance ratio (J) is the primary driver of these coefficients:  

J\=nDP​u(1−wP​)​

However, at extremely low speeds or when n approaches zero, J becomes numerically unstable. To circumvent this in production code, it is recommended to use the advance angle (ϵ) formulation, which remains stable across all four quadrants of operation :  

ϵ\=arctan2(uP​,0.7πnDP​)

In this representation, YP∗​(ϵ) can be modeled as a continuous function (typically a polynomial or a piecewise spline) that accounts for ahead, astern, and stopping conditions.  

#### Point Force vs. Independent Moment Modeling

A key research question for 3DOF engines is whether NP​ should be derived strictly from YP​. Theoretically, if YP​ is a lateral force applied at the propeller position xP​, then NP​\=xP​⋅YP​. However, as the transverse force is largely a result of distributed pressure changes along the hull's stern, the "effective" center of effort may not coincide with the propeller hub.  

For a high-fidelity simulator, it is recommended to model YP​ and NP​ as independent empirical terms. This provides the flexibility to tune the yaw-to-sway ratio, which is critical for replicating the correct "pivot point" behavior during docking. In a compromise model with limited data, applying YP​ at a modified longitudinal position xP​ (often slightly forward of the actual hub) can provide a computationally efficient approximation of the distributed moment.  

### Dependencies and Operational Variables

The intensity and direction of prop-walk are highly sensitive to the vessel's operational parameters. A realistic physics engine must calculate these forces dynamically at each time step.

#### Propeller Handedness and Rotation

The sign of the transverse force is strictly tied to the propeller's handedness. Most single-screw vessels use right-handed propellers, which rotate clockwise when viewed from aft in forward gear.  

| Propeller Handedness | Rotation (n\>0) | Stern Walk (Ahead) | Stern Walk (Astern) |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Right-Handed (RH) | Clockwise | To Starboard | To Port |
| Left-Handed (LH) | Counter-Clockwise | To Port | To Starboard |

The implementation must ensure that the force YP​ flips sign when n changes from positive to negative, but the magnitude must also scale with the slipstream intensity.  

#### Ahead vs. Astern Disparity

One of the most critical requirements for docking realism is the asymmetry between ahead and astern prop-walk. In forward gear, the transverse force is relatively weak—typically 3-4% of the axial thrust—because the hull does not "trap" the rotating slipstream. In reverse, the force can increase to 10-20% of the thrust as the slipstream is projected directly against the hull's afterbody.  

The simulator's logic should reflect this by using different coefficient magnitudes for the ahead (n\>0) and astern (n<0) quadrants. This ensures that "backing" behavior is corrected without significantly altering the validated "ahead" handling.  

#### Influence of Inflow and Drift Angle

The local drift angle at the propeller position (βP​) alters the asymmetric loading on the blades. As the ship sways or yaws, the water enters the propeller disk at an angle, effectively changing the P-factor. The local drift angle is calculated as:  

βP​\=arctan2(−(v+xP​r),u)

The coefficient YP∗​ should be adjusted by a factor that accounts for βP​, ensuring that the prop-walk intensity is physically coupled to the vessel's motion through the water.  

### Compromise Model for Limited Data Scenarios

For developers implementing this in a production environment with limited vessel-specific hydrodynamic data, a "Heuristic-Empirical" model is recommended. This model relies on a few tunable parameters that can be adjusted to match the expected behavior of a specific vessel class (e.g., a VLCC vs. a coastal tug).

#### The Primary "Transverse Percentage" Model

In this compromise model, YP​ is calculated as a fraction of the current longitudinal thrust XP​. This ensures that the lateral force always scales correctly with engine power and propeller loading.  

YP​\=XP​⋅Ctrans​⋅sign(n)

Recommended default values for Ctrans​:

*   **Ahead (n\>0):** 0.03 to 0.05 (3-5% of thrust).  
    
*   **Astern (n<0):** 0.10 to 0.15 (10-15% of thrust).  
    

The yaw moment NP​ is then derived using an effective moment arm xP​, which is typically set to roughly 45-50% of the vessel's length between perpendiculars (Lpp​) from the center of gravity.

The `k_decay` term is essential for ensuring that the prop-walk does not persist at high cruising speeds, where it would unnaturally destabilize the vessel.  

### Sign Conventions and Numerical Stability

In an MMG implementation, unambiguous sign conventions are critical to prevent "opposite-world" behavior where the ship walks the wrong way.  

#### Standard MMG Coordinate System

*   **X-axis:** Positive towards the bow.
    
*   **Y-axis:** Positive towards the starboard.
    
*   **Yaw (r):** Positive for clockwise rotation (bow to starboard).
    
*   **Propeller RPM (n):** Positive for forward rotation.
    
*   **Rudder Angle (δ):** Positive for deflection to starboard (initiating a port turn).  
    

#### Prop-Walk Sign Logic (Right-Handed Propeller)

For a RH propeller, the internal logic in the JavaScript engine should be:

1.  **Forward Gear (n\>0):**
    
    *   YP​ is positive (stern moves to starboard).
        
    *   NP​ is negative (bow yawing to port).  
        
2.  **Reverse Gear (n<0):**
    
    *   YP​ is strongly negative (stern moves to port).
        
    *   NP​ is strongly positive (bow yawing to starboard).  
        

If the vessel is marked as LH, these results are simply multiplied by -1.  

#### Addressing the Singularity at n = 0

One common pitfall in porting Python models to JavaScript is the division-by-zero error when the propeller stops spinning. When n\=0, the advance ratio J becomes infinite. The simulation should include a threshold check:  

JavaScript

```
if (Math.abs(n) < 0.01) {
    Y_P = 0;
    N_P = 0;
} else {
    // Calculate coefficients based on J or epsilon
}
```

This ensures that the transverse force naturally dissipates as the propeller stops, matching the physical reality of a vessel in "drift" mode.  

### Validation Scenarios for Low-Speed Realism

To confirm that the prop-walk implementation is functioning as intended, three primary validation scenarios should be executed and compared against professional maritime handbooks.

#### The "Stern Kick" Static Test

The vessel starts at a dead stop (u\=0,v\=0,r\=0) with zero rudder angle. A burst of "Full Astern" power is applied.  

*   **Success Criterion:** The vessel must immediately begin to yaw (starboard for RH prop) and sway laterally (port for RH prop) before it has moved backward significantly.  
    
*   **Quantitative Benchmark:** The stern should deviate from its original track by roughly 10-13% of the ship's length within the first few seconds of backing.  
    

#### The Back-and-Fill (Pivoting) Maneuver

The user attempts to turn the vessel 180∘ in a space only slightly larger than the ship's length.  

*   **Success Criterion:** For a RH vessel, turning to starboard (using forward power with starboard rudder and reverse power to "kick" the stern to port) should be significantly faster and require a smaller area than a turn to port.  
    
*   **Physical Reason:** In a starboard turn, the astern prop-walk (stern to port) assists the rotation initiated by the rudder. In a port turn, the prop-walk fights the rudder, making the maneuver sluggish or impossible in tight quarters.  
    

#### Speed Decay and Rudder Compensation

The vessel is accelerated to a steady forward speed (e.g., 5 knots).  

*   **Success Criterion:** The amount of rudder deflection required to maintain a straight course (compensating for YP​ and NP​ in forward gear) should be minimal (typically < 3 degrees) and should decrease as the vessel's speed u increases relative to the propeller speed n.  
    
*   **Validation:** If the ship requires excessive rudder to go straight at high speed, the `k_decay` or YP,ahead∗​ coefficient is too high.
    

### Advanced Considerations: Rudder Angle and Environmental Effects

While the primary prop-walk effect is a hull-propeller interaction, the rudder can modulate this force depending on its position.  

#### Rudder Interaction in Reverse

When a single-screw vessel is in reverse, the rudder is located _upstream_ of the propeller slipstream. Consequently, the rudder's effectiveness is drastically reduced compared to forward motion. However, the prop-walk itself remains relatively constant regardless of rudder angle, as the slipstream is projected forward _away_ from the rudder.  

For the simulator, it is accurate to model YP​ as independent of δ in reverse. This creates the realistic challenge where a pilot cannot "steer" their way out of a prop-walk in reverse until the vessel has gained significant sternway and the hull's hydrodynamic sway forces (YH​) begin to dominate.  

#### Influence of Water Depth (Shallow Water Effects)

Prop-walk is notoriously more pronounced in shallow water, such as in harbors or near piers. The presence of the seabed restricts the flow of water under the hull, forcing more of the rotating slipstream to interact with the stern's vertical surfaces.  

While a basic simulator may neglect depth, a high-fidelity implementation can scale the YP∗​ coefficient based on the h/T (water depth to draft) ratio. As h/T approaches 1.2, the transverse force can increase by as much as 50-100%, reflecting the "paddle-wheel" effect's intensification in confined waters.
